\section*{Jacobi Symbol}
The Jacobi Symbol gives up on the direct connection of being a quadratic residue, generalizing it instead.
It also retains nice Legendre-like properties, including multiplcativity and a Quadratic-reciprocity like
statement. Note that this means that Jacobi symbols are as easy to compute as Legendre symbols.

\begin{definition}
The \textit{Jacobi symbol} is defined as follows: for $n = p_1^{\alpha_1} p_2^{\alpha_2} \cdots p_k^{\alpha_k}$
\[
	\legendre{a}{n} = \legendre{a}{p_1}^{\alpha_1} \legendre{a}{p_2}^{\alpha_2} \cdots \legendre{a}{p_k}^{\alpha_k}.
\]
\end{definition}

\section*{Solovay-Stressen Primality Test}
Note that in general, the Euler criterion does \textit{not} hold for Jacobi symbol mod composite $n$.
For example, $\legendre{5}{21}\not\equiv 5^{\frac{21-1}{2}}\equiv 16\Mod{21}$, $16$ is not even $\pm 1$.
To check if $N$ is prime, compute $\legendre{a}{N}$ and $a^\frac{N-1}{2}\Mod{N}$ for some random values $a$.
If we find a value $a$ for which $\legendre{a}{N}\not\equiv a^{\frac{N-1}{2}}\Mod{n}$, we know that $N$
is not prime. 
\begin{fact}
	If $N$ is composite, then at least half of all $a\Mod{n}$ that are coprime to $N$ will give a failure of the Euler Criterion.
\end{fact}
This gives us a probabilistic test, and the probability of error is at most $\frac{1}{2}$.
However, calculating $\legendre{a}{N}$ requires factoring $N$, which would then already give us whether or not $N$
is prime. We can use the ``Quadratic-reciprocity'' like property of Jacobi symbols to get around this and calculate it directly
without ever factoring $N$.

\begin{definition}
	The \textit{primitive roots} $\Mod{m}$ are the numbers $a$ such that $\gcd(a, m) = 1$ and $a^{\phi(m)}\equiv 1\Mod{m}$.
	In other words, it is coprime to $m$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
	Let $(a, m)=1$. The \textit{order} of $a$ modulo $m$ denoted $\ord_m(a)$ is the smallest $k$ such that $a^k\equiv 1\Mod{m}$.
	Note that $\ord_m(a)\leq\varphi(m)$ .
\end{definition}
